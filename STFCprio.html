The STFC Prioritisation Outcome

What the Numbers Really Mean

A closer look at the 2026 STFC prioritisation outcome: why the headline 2.7% PPAN cash reduction masks a much larger loss of real research capacity, and what the cuts mean for laboratories, facilities and UK scientific capability.

On the 9th of July 2026, UKRI and STFC made public the first conclusions of a prioritisation exercise that took place over several months. This long waiting period kept the entire PPAN community in a complete uncertainty regarding the level of its funding for the next four years to come, damaging deeply the level of confidence that PPAN early-career researchers had in their potential career in the UK and leading the vast majority of ECRs to consider leaving the country. The goal of this article is to disentangle the narrative put forward by UKRI to present things in a positive manner from the hard reality that PPAN scientists, and in particular early-career researchers, can already see happening in their different fields of research.

The STFC prioritisation outcome is easiest to misunderstand if one starts with the headline cash figures: Indeed, the most visible official number is a 2.7% fall in the PPAN science budget, which Sir Ian Chapman (CEO of UKRI) himself described in front of the Science and Technology Committee as "not a big cut". But the practical picture is much harsher: a major real-terms loss of PPAN research capacity, a 58% reduction in the National Laboratories component, 20% cuts to STFC's Particle Physics Department and the UK Astronomy Technology Centre, and a 15% reduction to multidisciplinary facilities.

The reality behind the headline

This is not best understood as a 2.7% adjustment to PPAN. That number is a cash comparison between 2025/26 and 2029/30. It does not measure the amount of science, staff time, technical support or project capacity that the same budget can buy after several years of rising costs.

Using the inflation assumption in the independent projection based on UKRI's own table, the PPAN line is closer to a 19–20% real-terms contraction relative to an inflation-adjusted 2025/26 baseline. At the same time, the official package contains much deeper cuts to specific parts of STFC's research infrastructure and technical capability.

1. What the cuts mean in practice

The common thread across the prioritisation package is a reduction in effective scientific capacity: fewer resources for projects, reduced technical support, reduced accelerator and technology activity, less compute, less instrument development, reduced facility access, and pressure on the specialist teams that make these capabilities possible.

For PPAN, the official cash line falls only modestly, but a flat-or-falling cash budget over several years of inflation buys substantially less research. Protecting postdoctoral numbers at the 2025/26 level is valuable, but that baseline already follows a 15% cash reduction in grant funding relative to the earlier 2024 position discussed during the crisis. Protecting the number of postdocs therefore does not, by itself, protect the project support, technical capacity or career pipeline around them.

Elsewhere, the cuts are explicit and severe: the National Laboratories component falls by 58%, the Particle Physics Department and UK Astronomy Technology Centre by 20%, and multidisciplinary facilities by 15%. These numbers translate into reduced running time, lower user support, cuts to compute and accelerator programmes, mothballing of CLARA, pressure on instrument development, and possible loss of some laser and muon capabilities if alternative funding cannot be found.

2. Why the official “2.7%” PPAN figure is not the full story

The 2.7% number is a cash-term comparison between 2025/26 and 2029/30. It is not an inflation-adjusted measure of research capacity. On UKRI’s own table, PPAN moves from £199m to £193m in cash terms.1

This is why community analyses can reasonably describe the PPAN settlement as closer to a 20% real-terms contraction, even while UKRI describes it as a 2.7% cash reduction.

3. What UKRI/STFC officially announced

UKRI’s explainer states that STFC’s core budget remains broadly flat, but that rising energy costs, labour costs, foreign-exchange pressures affecting international subscriptions such as CERN, and expansion of activities during the previous spending review period mean that STFC cannot continue its current portfolio unchanged.1

The official package has several headline elements:

  • Discovery research / PPAN: the PPAN science budget falls from £199m in 2025/26 to £193m in 2029/30, which UKRI presents as a 2.7% cash decrease. UKRI says post-doctoral researchers within PPAN grants will be protected at the financial-year 2025/26 level using the 1.88% year-on-year inflation assumption set by HM Treasury during the spending review.1
  • Multidisciplinary facilities: Diamond Light Source, ISIS Neutron and Muon Source, and the Central Laser Facility remain operational, but the facilities budget falls by 15% over four years. UKRI says this will involve efficiency savings and choices to prioritise critical capability.1
  • International subscriptions: existing international subscriptions are maintained in full, even though those costs are expected to increase by 19%.1
  • National Laboratories and Estates: the combined budget falls by 8%, but within that aggregate the National Laboratories component falls by 58%, while Estates rises by 27% due to critical legacy work.2
  • Gateways: UKRI will assess progress in 2027 and 2028. If the programme does not meet targets, further action could include the closure of a multidisciplinary facility.1

5. The deepest structural cut is in the National Laboratories component

The most striking number in the official documents is the 58% reduction in the National Laboratories component within the National Laboratories and Estates budget. This is partly masked by the fact that the combined line falls by only 8%, because Estates is forecast to rise by 27%.2

The announced measures include: reducing accelerator and technology support by £8m per annum by 2029, including mothballing CLARA; reducing compute by £10m per annum; reducing instrument and technology development to realise at least £5.5m of savings; challenging RAL Space and Hartree to become cost-neutral to STFC; cutting Boulby Underground Mine’s operational budget by 40%; and prioritising only the most critical building work across the estate.1

This should not be read as a simple efficiency exercise. The listed measures affect capability: accelerator development, compute access, instrument and technology development, underground laboratory operation, and the financial model of major national laboratory units.

6. Multidisciplinary facilities: kept open, but with reduced capability

The package avoids immediate closure of Diamond, ISIS or the Central Laser Facility, but the official explainer says the multidisciplinary facilities budget falls by 15% and that STFC will reduce user support and technical assistance, reduce running time at ISIS, and seek alternative funding or operating models for the Lasers for Science Facility and the muon beamlines at ISIS; if that cannot be found, those capabilities may close.1

The FT reported industry concern about the effect of cuts on Diamond and related facilities, including concern from chemicals and materials users that reduced access to such infrastructure could damage UK innovation capability.5

7. The political and governance context

The House of Commons Science, Innovation and Technology Committee published a statement after receiving the Chapman–Dougherty letter. It notes the 2.7% PPAN reduction, the 15% multidisciplinary facilities reduction, more than £100m of additional UKRI support to avoid sharp transitions, and the fact that changes “may have an impact on roles”. Dame Chi Onwurah welcomed greater clarity but said the committee remained deeply concerned and that, given rising science spend and commitments to curiosity-driven research, “these cuts shouldn’t be happening.”3

Separately, Times Higher Education reported Onwurah’s criticism of changes to STFC Council minutes, including her description of “extraordinary doctoring” and governance concerns about substantive changes to how the funding shift was recorded.4

8. An objective reading

A fair reading needs to hold two things together.

First, UKRI/STFC are not presenting this as an ideological abandonment of PPAN. Their line is that STFC has a flat core budget, rising cost pressures, a need to reach financial balance by 2029/30, and a package designed to preserve critical capability while avoiding immediate sharp transitions.1

Second, the numbers describe a real contraction in capability. The official package contains a 15% cut to multidisciplinary facilities, a 58% cut to National Laboratories activity, a 20% cut to STFC’s Particle Physics Department and UK Astronomy Technology Centre, reduced project support in PPAN, reduced compute, reduced accelerator and technology support, a possible future facility closure if gateway targets fail, and a PPAN cash budget that falls despite inflation.1

The key question is therefore not whether the cuts exist. They plainly do. The key question is whether UKRI and DSIT are accurately describing the loss of capability created by these decisions, and whether Parliament and the community accept that the damage is a reasonable price for balancing STFC’s budget inside the present allocation.

9. A concise formulation

The STFC prioritisation outcome is best understood as a package that protects some headline commitments while reducing the operating capacity underneath them. It maintains subscriptions, avoids immediate closure of the main multidisciplinary facilities, and protects PPAN postdoctoral numbers at the 2025/26 baseline. But it also cuts capability in national laboratories, facilities, centres, computing, accelerator and technology support, and parts of the PPAN project portfolio.

This is why a community response should not focus only on whether “PPAN is down 2.7%”. The more important question is what happens to the people, projects, technical capability and international credibility that make UK PPAN and STFC-supported science function.
